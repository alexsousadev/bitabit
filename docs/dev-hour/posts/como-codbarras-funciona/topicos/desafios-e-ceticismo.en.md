# Implementation Challenges and Initial Skepticism

Like any new technology, the introduction of the barcode faced strong initial skepticism from various sectors of society:

- **Consumers**: Questioned the accuracy of the registered prices.

    > They thought: *'If the price isn't stickered on the product, how will I know if the machine charged me the right amount at checkout?'*. There was a real fear that supermarkets would alter prices in the central computer without the customer noticing.

- **Labor Unions**: Feared that the machines would cause mass unemployment.

    > Unions realized that optical scanning would speed up checkout lines and eliminate the need for employees to stamp prices on every single product in inventory.

- **Retailers and Manufacturers**: Were reluctant to invest in scanners for products that didn't yet have the code printed on the packaging.

    > Implementing the barcode required a massive investment. Supermarket owners had to buy expensive central computers and laser scanners that were still state-of-the-art technology. Manufacturers, in turn, had to change their production lines and packaging designs to print the code. No one wanted to take the first step and risk losing money.

- **General Population**: There was a fear that ["Big Brother" (1984 - George Orwell)](https://share.google/aimode/tBDfyyY5aR02ICXQZ) would use the technology to monitor and track citizens' lives.

- **Conspiracy Theories**: Some groups, influenced by religious books of the time like *Mary Stewart Relf's "The New Money System 666"*, claimed that the codes concealed the number `666`, representing the biblical "Mark of the Beast".

    > As previously explained, the UPC barcode has three longer guard bars (at the beginning, middle, and end) that only serve to guide the scanner's laser. It so happens that, visually, the pattern of these three bars is identical to the pattern the system uses to read the number 6. For religious groups of the time, seeing a structure that seemed to embed 6-6-6 in a system designed specifically for "buying and selling" was the perfect fit for the apocalyptic prophecy of the Mark of the Beast.
